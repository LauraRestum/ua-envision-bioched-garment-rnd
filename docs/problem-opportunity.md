# Problem & Opportunity

## The Problem

Bio/chemical protective garments require multi-layer systems that create compounding tradeoffs:

- **Protection vs. mobility** — higher protection increases weight and restricts movement
- **Slow iteration cycles** — current development pipelines are not built for rapid prototyping
- **Material innovation outpacing integration** — advanced materials exist but lack a defined pathway into functional, manufacturable garment systems

The result: the gap between what's technically possible in the lab and what gets deployed in the field remains wide, and closes slowly.

## The Core Tension

Material innovation is not the bottleneck. The bottleneck is translating that innovation into a system that can be prototyped, tested, evaluated, and scaled — fast enough to be relevant.

## The Opportunity

Build a model where each party handles what they're actually equipped to do:

- Under Armour develops advanced materials suited for bio/chem protection
- Envision integrates those materials into functional garment systems and produces prototypes rapidly
- The government funds and evaluates through OTA, with a clear use case and defined team

This removes the translation gap. UA doesn't have to become a manufacturer. Envision doesn't have to develop materials from scratch. The government gets a credible team with a defined prototype pathway.

## Why Now

OTA funding exists specifically for rapid prototyping in cases like this. The mechanism is available. What's been missing is a team structured to use it — one that covers material innovation, integration, and manufacturing without requiring the government to coordinate them independently.
