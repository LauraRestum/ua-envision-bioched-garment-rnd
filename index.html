Opening 01 / 07
UA × Envision × DEVCOM

Material innovation
stalls at integration.

Under Armour develops the materials. Envision integrates them into functional garment systems. The government funds and evaluates through OTA. That's the model.

Problem 02 / 07
The Bottleneck

The bottleneck is integration,
not material innovation.

Bio/chem protection requires multi-layer systems. Current development pipelines can't iterate fast enough. Material innovation is ahead of the manufacturing and integration capacity to deploy it.

The gap between what's possible in the lab and what gets deployed in the field is wide — and closes slowly.

Model 03 / 07
Integrated Roles

An integrated system,
not a handoff model.

Each party is responsible for what they're structurally equipped to do. UA supplies material R&D. Envision integrates and prototypes. DEVCOM evaluates and funds.

The system works because the roles don't overlap — and the gaps between them are closed by design.

Pathway 05 / 07
Execution Sequence

OTA → Prototype →
Evaluation → Scale

OTA is the fastest viable entry point at this stage.

The concept is integration-ready — not raw research — which fits OTA's mandate directly. Envision builds the functional garment system. DEVCOM evaluates against defined requirements. Pilot manufacturing follows.

Why It Works 06 / 07
Structural Advantage

Speed. Integration.
Risk reduction.

This partnership is structurally superior because capability fit and pre-coordination eliminate the delays that come with assembling a team after a contract is awarded.

Speed
Envision compresses prototype timelines. Rapid cut-and-sew prototyping means iteration cycles are measured in weeks, not quarters.
Integration
Materials and manufacturing are pre-coordinated. UA's material output feeds directly into Envision's production process — no translation layer required.
Risk Reduction
The government evaluates one integrated team, not three separate vendors. Defined accountability. Reduced coordination overhead.
